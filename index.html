Why I Fired My $2,000/Year Booking App

For six years, I worked a "Shadow Shift."

I’d finish a full day of wrenching at my mobile bike repair business, Bicycle Repair Hub, wash the grease off my hands, and sit down at 7:00 PM to start my second job: Calendar Tetris.

When you are a small startup, you keep expenses low so you can keep your profit high. No secret, just math. This meant sitting down to set up appointments for everyone that had emailed, texted, or called that day. But before I could do that, I had to confirm (or not) the appointments I had set up the previous day to make sure today's appointments didn't conflict. Just writing that is confusing. But that is what it was in the early days. Back and forth, night after night, year after year.

I did this for about 6 years before finally finding the software that I thought would be my time and sanity saviour. The price tag? Over $2,000. Yikes! But it was cheaper than hiring someone so I bit the bullet, subscribed, and implemented it on my website. It was a HUGE time saver—at first.

The key was geolocation. Because I drive to my clients, I couldn't use a regular appointment app. I'd be driving from the deep SE to NW all day long losing efficiency (another profit killer). I needed the software to group appointment slots based on location. This software did that. The thing it didn't do well was the "customer service" side. Reminders didn't send reliably. I'd show up, and clients wouldn't be there or wouldn't remember the booking. I was paying a "SaaS Tax" for software that was making my life harder, not easier.

I stopped working for "the man" because I wanted to do things on my own terms. This software locked me into problems that I had to live with and that didn't sit well with me. The solution? Build it myself. From scratch. Hundreds of lines of code later, I have a system that is BETTER than the one I paid $2,000 for.

This is The Base Protocol. I stopped renting my business logic and started owning it. To be fully self-sufficient, I had to own the tools and the time.
